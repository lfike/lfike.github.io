Friday 4/17/26

Landed at ICN at approximately 6:20am. It was the first time that the flight I was on has been on time all week. I waited for David, who was on a different flight, to land at about 8:00am. We were in different terminals and ended up taking different trains in to the city. I had a hard time navigating the transit system, and tried asking for help but could not find anyone who spoke English. In all of the other countries so far it was much easier to find someone who spoke English but here not so much. I took a train to the other airport, Gimpo, and from there a train to Yeomchang station. We were arriving much earlier than our check in time so we were going to drop our bags and then spend the day roaming around the city. The one way ticket I bought from the airport was supposedly going to take me the whole way; however, when I tried to scan out of the gates it blocked the way. I went up to he ticket return machine and realized that said I was 100 Won short, which isn't a lot of money, but I didn't yet have any cash on me. I had to call the attendant and thankfully he spoke English and just let me through.

For the next hour or so I drug my suitcase around while I looked for an ATM that accepted my card. I finally found a Woori bank and withdrew 100,000 Won, about $70 USD. After this I took a bus to what my map told me was the address of the hotel, but I ended up at a totally different place than David who was already there. I ended up just calling an Uber taxi to take me there which was like $6 USD.

The hotel didn't have anyone at the desk and we found a kiosk that was used for check in with everyone's bags just shoved in to the corner there. We did the same and left the hotel in search of somewhere to eat. There was a ramen restaurant nearby, yes not much of Korean restaurant, but it was close and we were hungry, and it was really good.

After we ate we took the train to the Korean War Museum. The museum was huge and had some amazing exhibits. I did not know before going the extent of the US's involvement in the war, so that was very interesting to learn. There were trucks and other vehicles that you could climb up in to the seat of, as well as an assortment of US and Korean aircraft. Inside they had three floors of exhibits with relics from all of the allied forces on display.

Following this, we went to a Korean restaurant and had bulgogi. What we were not prepared for was how much extra stuff was going to be brought out when we thought we were just getting beef and rice. We asked the server for help in what to eat and how, and she briefly described it to us, but too be honest it was really difficult and we didn't think it tasted all that good.

After this we made a quick stop at a convenient store for some snacks and went to the hotel. The hotel was nothing special with my only real complaint being that the bathroom door didn't have a handle nor could it close all the way. But we were both exhausted and just showered and crashed for the night.

Saturday 4/18/26

We checked out of the hotel at 7am as today we are going to Busan, South Korea. This is the second biggest city in South Korea. Getting to the airport from our hotel and through security took only about 30 minutes in total, a much better experience than some of the larger airports we've been to. We had a perfect day to fly and to explore Busan. The flight was really quick, less than an hour, and on time which is always nice.

We took the light rail from the airport to the main train station and hopped on a train to Nampo station. Then we crossed a huge bridge to get to the side of the river that our hotel was on. I believe it was called Hotel Adela. We dropped our bags there and started walking around the hotel. We went on a big pier that had a bunch of fishing and tug boats parked there and it was neat to see those big boats come in and out of there. We continued to wander around and saw this cool sign that said the distances to a bunch of major cities worldwide, even Pyongyang, North Korea.

We ended up at a restaurant serving Korean food, and I had glass noodles and a Coke, along with all of the sides that were given out with the meal, which was pretty common everywhere that we went. The food was pretty good, but Korean food is not my favorite I don't think.

After that we went to the Busan tower and it was a pretty reasonable 10,000 Won to go to the top. The landscape here is probably my favorite with the big hills with neighborhoods built up them, there was always something to look at.

Once we got to the bottom, we checked out the cafe that was there and were both drawn to the his heaping helping of mangos and ice cream on a Belgian waffle.

From here we took a train and then a small bus in to the mountains to a place that we were going to watch the sun set over the city from. We got off at the last stop on the bus line and then looked up at the daunting slope wondering if we had enough time to make it up to the top before it was dark. We helplessly looked around trying to find a trail for a few minutes until a man offered to help us. We used Google translate to hear the directions he was giving us and he pointed us in the right direction and said it was about 200m. Whether that was elevation gain or total distance we're still not sure.

Honestly, it was a pretty brutal hike! Once we got to the top though it was definitely worth it. The sun peeked out briefly as it was going to down.

After that hike, we were both starving and we ordered an Uber down the mountain back towards the hotel, and our driver even let us get out of the car to quickly take more pictures of the city from a different outpost.

Finding something to eat was a little more difficult than it should have been, but the first place we wanted to go to was closed. I ended up eating a spicy chicken rice bowl, and David had fried chicken. That was it for the night and tomorrow we are going to Gyeongju by train, a smaller sized city just, just for the day.

Sunday 4/19/26

My day started at 6am and after getting out of the shower I opened the shades to see an awesome view from the hotel room.

We arrived to Busan station where we are boarding a train at approximately 8am. The train was pretty quiet for the most part — neither of us had anyone sitting next to us.

Arriving at Gyengju station.

Then we took a bus in to the city and decided on where we were going to start our day from.

We ended up touring various sites, including these h huge memorial mounds, temples, bell, and museum. After a bit of walking, we needed a break and stopped at a 7-Eleven for a snack.

This town was a nice change of pace from the huge mega-cities that we have been visiting. Everything slowed down and made the day more enjoyable! We took a long bus ride to the Bulguksa Temple, the most famous temple in Korea, where we spent the next hour and a half or so.

Now I know we are in Korea, but I was reading on Reddit for food recommendations, and many people said that the best pizza in all of Asia was here in Gyeongju, and we decided we had to try it. The restaurant was called [INSERT] and although it said they were open on NAVER, the Google maps equivalent for Korea, they were in between their lunch and dinner times and were not seating anyone. We were a little bummed, and I'm not sure if I looked that way or what, but the owner spoke a little English and told us he would remember us if we came back at 5pm and let us eat which was really nice of him. In the meantime we killed a little time by checking out a local thrift store, which actually had some really interesting, quality clothes at good prices but we had absolutely no way of checking that stuff home in our bags, especially the nice winter jacket I liked. We tried McDonalds, just for a snack and to see if they had any local foods on the menu, which they didn't, but I had some fries which were pretty good. Then we went to a Samsung store which had everything from phones to computers to washers to upright closet steamer things. A lot of this stuff we don't get in the US, or at least it's not common at all.

When we returned to the restaurant we saw a huge line of probably 30 people sitting outside and were a little concerned that we may not get to eat because we had to leave to go to the train station in just about 2 hours. To my surprise, he came out and told us to follow him in and let us sit right behind the prep area as the first table to sit down for dinner that night. Basically we skipped the whole line which was very nice of him to do for us. David ordered the margarita pizza and I had the salami for myself, alone with a local beer that was pineapple tasting. The pizza was excellent and it was awesome to get to watch him make all of the pizzas up too!

From here we went to a gelato place that was neighboring the pizza place, and I had fig and cream and Korean melon as my two flavors, along with a small sample of the chocolate. They were all excellent and were each very unique.

This was unfortunately our last stop and we took the bus back to the main station there.

The train was a few minutes late and I took a video of the whole thing coming in. This time it was busier than earlier.

Once we got back I was still fairly hungry and searched out some fried chicken. I ended up being served, to my surprise, what I think was a whole chicken, along with shrimp crackers, pickled radish, and then a variety of sauces including a wasabi sauce, barbecue like sauce, and then plain salt. I ate the vast majority of it and it was really good I was not disappointed at all.

This was our last day in Busan, and I went to bed pretty late despite having to get up early to take our flight back to Seoul. I packed my bag as much as I could for the night as David was going to leave it at the desk in the morning if I ended up getting up early enough to the Jagalchi fish market.

Monday 4/20/26

I was able to get myself up in the morning and headed over to the Jagalchi market, the biggest fish market in all of Korea. The premise of the place is really to pick out what you want to eat on the first floor and then they take it upstairs and cook it for you however you want. I was still absolutely stuffed from the chicken I had the night before and didn't end up ordering anything.

David and I met up and continued to stroll around the what seemed like never ending streets of markets. I ducked in to a tackle store to see if I could find a Korean made bait or something for Dad but it was all stuff for big water. Bummer! Lastly we went to a camera store and checked out the gear they had for sale before heading to the airport to fly back to Seoul. This flight boarded and left a bit later than the other, but I slept the whole way back.

When we landed in Seoul it was about 15° cooler there than in Busan, and pretty rainy and wet.

We took the train to a restaurant called Gold Pig, a Korean BBQ spot, to put our names on the waitlist. To our surprise there was no wait, but we weren't hungry, so we left and gambled that when we came back the wait wouldn't be too long. From here we took an Uber to the hotel. We stayed at the Mondrian, a very very nice hotel that David was able to get a deal on with his credit card. After checking in we explored the area for a little bit before heading to back to the restaurant. When we showed up there were 20 groups in front of us! So while we waited we went to a grocery store, where I found it cool to see some of the specialty stuff and ingredients that I buy back home just like in a regular grocery store. They also had Dr. Pepper in a can which I obviously had to try.

I'm not a coffee drinker, but there was one of the Starbucks Reserve branches just like tucked away in a random neighborhood that we stumbled upon. There were no flashy signs or advertisements.

By this point we got a notification that we jumped from 9th in line to the point where we could come in; however, we were like 15 minutes walking back. We jogged part of the way there and made it just barely in time to keep our spot. The whole experience was very overwhelming, and not knowing the language made it difficult to follow the guy cooking our food at the table. All in all though it was good food, mostly pork but some beef, that was cooked and served with sides right at our table.

Among the sides was Kimchi stew, which they eventually added noodles to make it a ramen dish.

With our reservation we got 140,000 Won of credit to spend at the hotel bars and restaurant. All of the drinks were pretty extravagant and nothing I would normally order, but neat to try.

After this we went upstairs to another bar, where I had a honey something cocktail, not really sure. It was really strong, but hey it was free and the bar was fascinating. David and I just talked and sipped the drinks for a while.

Tuesday 4/21/26

Today was the day we both were really waiting for which was a tour to the DMZ. We had breakfast at the hotel, which was easily the best hotel breakfast I have ever had.

Then we took the transit to the tour meeting point and were introduced to our tour guide Julie Park. She was a local from Seoul and despite looking like she was 25, was actually in her mid 30s and was the most tenured tour guide at the company. She was really knowledgeable and passionate about the stops that we made and gave us all of the necessary history. She explained to us that there are really three defining lines between North and South Korea. The NLL, SLL, and MLL. The MLL was the actual border, in the middle a 4km wide strip, each country getting 2km. We were crossing the SLL, in to the DMZ on the tour today. The history of the Korean War I was largely unfamiliar with. I did not know that the US withdrew their statement of protection of South Korea, which meant that North Korea and alliances had the opportunity to invade. They took turns taking cities and losing them again, before a truce was established, not a peace treaty, meaning that to this day they are still at war. When we got close to the border, there was a checkpoint where a SK soldier came on the bus to check our passports and get a headcount.

There were a series of tunnels dig by North Korea under the border which have been found, and we went in the third one. It was very far underground and we were allowed to go right up to the border. These places were all UN stops and we could not take any photos or videos most of the way. They wouldn't even let you carry your phone or have a watch on.

At this stop they also had a souvenir shop so I picked up a patch, keychain, and postcard. Although if I would have been checking a bag back to Singapore I would have bought a piece of the original wire fence on the border — not sure they would have let me through with that especially in China.

From her we actually went to the border and had a perfectly clear view across the border. Again photography here was limited, with shooting pictures across the border strictly prohibited. The old military observatory which is no longer used was right outside the bus.

Then we walked up to the new observatory.

Again off the to left in this picture was the final boundary river and then North Korea. There were two flag poles parallel to each other, one on each side. At the base of theirs though was a propaganda city, yes a fake city, which had framed buildings but no movement, vehicles, or signs of any life. Also the SK flag was erected first at 100m, and then shortly after NK tried to one up them with a 160m flag pole, which actually has a Guinness world record. They had a huge room with glass windows that closely resembled a big school lecture hall that we sat at while our guide Julie explained everything that we could see. That included farmers working in a field, a very, very tall GPS and radio jamming tower so that SK media could not propagate over the border, and the Joint Security Area (JSA) where it used to be allowed to actually cross in to North Korea in a small room that has soil of both countries in it. This is where Kim Jong Un and Donald Trump met and shook hands and they both posed for a photo on the North Korean side of the border. This has been closed for three years because an American soldier, who was facing charges in South Korea, ran across the border. He was eventually caught and delivered back to the US where he is in prison. Once the JSA reopens, which it likely will under the more NK friendly president that just took office in SK, we Americans are able to do that. What we can never do, is actually visit North Korea on a state sponsored trip or for any reason at all. They do seldomly open their border to tourists for their propaganda tour in Pyongyang, but the guide pleaded with us that even if we could, like many other countries citizens can, to not go there. We were joined briefly by an actual North Korean defector, who shared her story of trading and bartering food, currency, and goods to fund her escape. She crossed in to China, and then with fake documentation, travelled to Laos and then Thailand and then eventually South Korea, where she is now much safer and the government has provided tons of resources to help her build a life here. Since then she was able to fund her one and only son's escape. Her husband though, was killed by in a prison there when they were arrested for not obtaining a proper permit to sell certain goods. Now though, she still lives fearfully and is hiring a bodyguard to stay with her at all times. She did say the one thing she misses about her home is that because of how underdeveloped it is, the air was so pure and fresh with vast wilderness unlike that which is being disturbed by humans and industry everywhere else in the world. It was a lot to take in and see knowing what has happened there. Opinions on reunification are currently mixed, but most of the South Koreans do not seem to be concerned with that right now at all. On the rooftop of the observatory there were binoculars to look further across the border. From here we went down to a park and were guided through the memorials there. I bought a 500 South Korean Won bill, as did David, and we were given those on the ride. I thought that was a pretty good souvenir.

The first was a big plague with the lyrics to a song that any sang when they were able to communicate with their families back home when they were split. I believe in the 80s there was one opportunity for people to send a single message and goodbye to their families that gained so much traction that it ran for weeks instead of the allotted hour it was given.

Next, when Japan tried colonize Korea they enslaved thousands of young Korean girls for “the pleasure of the Japanese military”, for which there were two sitting statues, each with their feet not touching the ground, a bird on one shoulder, and a shadow that made them look much older than they were to show what they endured was much beyond their age. To this day Japan has not offered an apology which Korea is still trying to solicit before the last of these women pass away. At this time there are only five who are still alive.

Next we were shown the locomotive of a train that was used to transport goods and troops, which in not 100% sure I heard this right, they had allied forces destroy before reaching its destination in enemy territory because they were tipped off an attack that was to take place when they got there.

From here we went to our last stop, a rope bridge over a lake that was a former battleground.

One thing our guide Julie was also very passionate about was skin care, which I guess is a big thing in Korea. She explained some of the stuff that most people use which was interesting because everyone did look much younger with really smooth skin.

From her went to the Nike Seoul store and even though it was pricey, custom made hoodies by picking out designs and taping them on the sweatshirt and then they would be printed on right there.

From here we went to one of the craziest libraries I have ever seen! It was just in a random mall and publicly accessible to everyone.

From here we went to try Bingsu, a Korean dessert that is essentially frozen milk that is shaved, like shaved ice, topped mixed with condensed milk and then other toppings. It was a huge helping and was very good, although as it started to melt lost the visual appeal it had at the start.

Wednesday 4/22/26

Today I got up at 5am and we both quickly packed and checked out before taking a 1hr 45 minute transit ride to the airport. We checked in and went through a pretty long security line, and then went to a tax refund kiosk to recoup the taxes we paid on the sweatshirts, because tourist purchases above a certain amount are tax refund eligible. We got a whole $4 USD back. Then we swung by a currency exchange and traded the rest of our Won for Yuan. Then we went to the gate. While boarding, I got pulled aside because of the current visa situation for US citizens in China, and they had to see and take a photo of my outbound ticket back to Singapore.

Eventually I boarded but the plane was pretty full by this point and I had to put my bag in the bin like six rows up. A lunch of chicken and potatoes or Bibimbap was served, and I took the chicken and some Coke. The airline we are one, Asiana, is about to be bought and merged with Korean Air which was cool that we got to fly with them before they are all gone.

The flight had the most turbulence I had ever experienced.
